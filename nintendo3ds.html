The Nintendo 3DS Is Still One of the Best Consoles Ever (Yeah, Even Now)

Posted May 10, 2025

The 3DS came out in 2011. It’s 2025 now. That’s 14 years of handheld greatness, and somehow, this little clamshell still holds up. While everyone’s chasing 4K graphics and cloud gaming, the 3DS just sits there like, “Yeah, I’ve got style.” And honestly? It kinda wins.

Let’s Be Real: It Does Stuff the Switch Still Doesn’t

Look, we love the Switch. But let’s not pretend it didn’t quietly kill off everything weird and fun about Nintendo’s older systems. The 3DS had:

  • Two screens. Actual dual-screen gameplay. Ever heard of innovation?
  • StreetPass, AKA a social feature that was actually fun and didn't try to sell you anything.
  • Backward compatibility with DS games. Imagine buying a new console and still being able to play your entire old library. Wild.
  • Built-in stylus support that didn’t require you to buy a separate $100 “pro pencil.”

Meanwhile, the Switch still can’t figure out folders.

The Game Library Is Unmatched

You want JRPGs? Got 'em. Platformers? Got 'em. Underrated weird Nintendo experiments? Oh yeah. The 3DS has one of the most diverse libraries out there—games like:

  • Kid Icarus: Uprising – chaotic, beautiful, and hilarious.
  • Fire Emblem: Awakening – the one that saved the franchise.
  • Pokémon X/Y, Sun/Moon – maybe not perfect, but undeniably iconic.
  • Luigi’s Mansion: Dark Moon, Majora’s Mask 3D, Animal Crossing: New Leaf — all bangers.
  • And, of course, the endless stream of hidden gems in the eShop back in the day.

There's so much range here that it's hard to be bored—even in 2025.

“Extra Features” That May or May Not Exist

We’re not saying you should do anything that voids your warranty (especially since Nintendo doesn’t even really support it anymore). But let’s just say the 3DS community has found some... creative ways to breathe new life into the system.

Some users, hypothetically, might install quality-of-life improvements. Or access “backups” of games they legally own. Or even unlock region-free play to enjoy stuff that never left Japan. Again, all totally theoretical and educational. Nothing to see here, Nintendo.

It Still Feels Like a Console Made With Heart

Maybe that’s what makes the 3DS stand out so much. It wasn’t trying to be a tablet. It wasn’t trying to be a hybrid media machine. It was just a handheld built to be fun—packed with charm, quirks, and the kind of polish Nintendo rarely applies to its UI anymore.

It had a personality. The Switch has better graphics, sure. But the 3DS has vibes.

Final Thoughts

If you’ve got a 3DS sitting in a drawer somewhere, charge it up. Give it a second life. Whether you’re diving back into old favorites or “exploring” some community enhancements, there’s still so much this little machine can do.

Fourteen years later and it still hits harder than half the stuff on the market today. So no, the 3DS isn’t dead. It’s just chilling—quietly being one of the greatest handhelds of all time.
